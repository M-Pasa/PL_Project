## Retrospective

**What I would change if I started over.** I would lift `<let-decl>` to a prefix section *from day one*, rather than placing it inside `<rules-section>` and then re-discovering at §4.7-implementation time that the P1 grammar made athlete-scope lets unreachable from workout and meal bodies. The §4.6.3 "in every expression that follows it" rule and the §4.6.5 athlete-scope visibility bullet were locally consistent and *jointly* incoherent — I caught this only after writing the §4.7 widening and trying to feed a `let benchBase = 80 kg` into an `exercise … weight:` slot; the let wasn't in the env yet. The fix (a new prefix `<let-section>` between `<routine-decl>`s and `<body-sections>`) was structurally clean but forced an AST edit, a field rename (`MacroDecl.quantity → value`), and updates to three sample programs. Starting over, I would draft §4.6 and §4.4 against an executable mental model — "where does the env actually contain this name when this slot is evaluated?" — instead of trusting two prose sections to compose.

**What did not work and got scoped out.** Two deliberate omissions. First, the §4.5.5 type algebra admits `Rate(D₁,D₂)` as a *kind* but not as the result of `Quantity / Time`, so `plateStep / 1 week` is a static error and rates must be written either as literals or as identifiers of an already-rate-typed let. The first draft of `valid_05_expressions.fl` failed on exactly this — I replaced the division with a let-bound rate literal rather than adding a §4.5 rule for one syntactic convenience. Second, `TargetDecl` lost source-unit fidelity in the P2 `--run` output (entry 13): once the threshold became an `<expression>` rather than a literal `<quantity>`, there is no "declared unit" to echo, only the canonical g value the interpreter computes — synthesising a display unit from the expression tree is more work than the display gain justifies.

**Single hardest design decision.** Whether to model `Rate(D₁,D₂)` as a separate type kind or as a derived form of `Quantity D₁ / Quantity D₂`. Choosing the kind (what I did) costs one §4.5 rule (Rate × Time → Quantity, §4.5.5) and the asymmetry above. Choosing the derivation would have unified rates with arithmetic, but it pulls in two further questions Sebesta §6.5 warns about: does every `Quantity / Time` evaluate to a rate (even ones the user did not mean as a rate, e.g. `bodyweight / 1 week`?), and is `Mass × Time` the inverse — which is nonsense in the domain. The kind-based answer keeps the type universe finite and listable, which the closed-lexicon design in §4.2.5 also depends on; the derivation-based answer would have rippled into both. I committed to the kind and accepted the syntactic asymmetry, and the §4.7 sample exercise (`valid_05_expressions.fl`) confirms the cost is small — five lines of let-bound rates instead of inline divisions.

## Self-Assessment (against §5 rubric)

**Correctness — A.** All stated behaviours work and the spec/implementation are consistent at submission: every §4.3 production is reachable from the five valid samples, every §4.5 rule is exercised by `invalid_typeerror.fl`'s ten diagnostics, every §4.4 operational rule (including §4.4.5 routine-call substitution and §4.4.8 multi-week unfolding) is exercised by the `--run` outputs in D3, and the §4.5 routine-parameter binding matches the interpreter's call-site substitution semantics (the type checker pushes each param as `Quantity(family)` into its env when walking the routine body, mirroring the runtime push). I do not know of a contradiction between spec and implementation at this submission.

**Design Justification — A.** Every major decision in §4.8 names the trade-off Sebesta would recognise (closed lexicon vs. user-named identifiers, name vs. structural equivalence for record kinds, single-assignment vs. mutable cells, static vs. dynamic scoping, kind-based vs. derivation-based rates). Each paragraph is one decision, one alternative, one cost, one payoff, in my own voice — which is exactly what the rubric's "Excellent" cell asks for.

**Use of Sebesta Framework — A.** Every design claim in D1 is anchored to a Sebesta section number (e.g. §6.5–§6.7 for record decisions, §7.2 for precedence, §7.5 for operand evaluation, §5.5.1 for static scoping, §3.5 for the SOS-style semantics in §4.4). The mapping table at the top of D1 (project §X.Y → Sebesta §a.b) was the first thing I wrote and I have not added a design claim that the table does not cover. Terminology — referencing environment, FIRST/FOLLOW, closed lexicon, name equivalence — is used in the technical sense Sebesta defines, not as decoration.

**Originality — A.** FitLang is one DSL with one domain spine (typed physical quantities tracked over time against a body goal) — not "fitness syntax bolted onto a generic scripting language". The integration shows up in three places the rubric's Excellent cell asks about: the §4.4 operational rules are domain-specific (a `<progress-stmt>` is *not* an assignment, it is a week-indexed unfolding), the §4.5 type system carries dimensions as first-class kinds (Mass/Energy cross-family rejection happens at parse-plus-check time), and `plan N week` is the unit of output rather than a single-snapshot evaluator — which is what makes `progress` and `goal lose/gain` load-bearing rather than ornamental.

**Code Quality — B.** The package separation is clean: `Lexer`, `Parser`, `AstDumper`, `TypeChecker`, `Interpreter`, `Main`, each in one file under `src/fitlang/`. The Interpreter's per-slot helpers (`evalMass`, `evalRateMassTime`) are honest downcasts that the type checker has already proved, which matches the design's "checker proves, interpreter computes" split. Two things keep this from an A: the §4.7 widening rippled into six declaration classes and the AST diff is wider than I would like (entry 13), and `TypeChecker.checkExpr` has grown a few long `instanceof` cascades that a visitor would clean up. Both are readable but not pretty.

**Exam Performance — C (provisional).** Exam 1 (2026-05-14) went badly — the parser and scope questions were the dominant share and I lost ground on details I should have had at the front of my mind (FIRST/FOLLOW intersections, the precise rule for `<rate>` look-ahead, the §5.5 static-scope referencing-environment construction). Exam 2 (2026-05-28) is cumulative-possible and the §4.4 / §4.5 material is fresh from implementation, which should help, but I am marking this honestly rather than predicting a recovery I have not earned yet. Targeted prep on the same parser/scope topics, plus the §4.5 rules I wrote from scratch in P2, is the plan for the week between submission and exam.
