## What is working

D1 is complete for the P1 sections (§4.1 overview, §4.2 lexical structure, §4.3 EBNF, §4.6 names/binding/scope/lifetime), with every design choice grounded in a Sebesta section and at least one real-language analogue. The §4.3 grammar is **LL(1)** by construction and now includes both §2-mandated extensions: a `<when-decl>` control structure dispatched on the static `<goal-mode>` value bound in `<athlete-params>`, and a `<routine-decl>` / `<routine-call>` pair that gives the language user-defined parameterised templates (the DSL-idiomatic version of "user-defined functions with parameters"). D2 ships a hand-written recursive-descent lexer and parser in Java that builds a typed AST, dumps it via `--dump-ast`, and produces single-line domain-specific error diagnostics (`samples\malformed_*.fl` show all five). The eight sample programs (3 valid, 5 malformed — D3) all parse with the expected outcomes; coverage of every reachable §4.3 nonterminal is recorded in D3 §1.4. The D4 journal carries six dated entries (2026-05-02 through 2026-05-08), including experiment **E1** comparing AI-generated EBNF from Opus 4.7, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and GPT-5.5 against §4.3 — the cross-model comparison turned up a real spec ambiguity (the `<rate>` slash-syntax) and three classes of model error caught before they would have polluted the design.

## What is hard

The closed-lexicon rule in §4.2.5 (every unquoted alphabetic lexeme is reserved-word, mode literal, or unit; `IDENT` only inside `let` and routine parameters) keeps falling out of step with my own additions: every new construct tempts me to add a fresh keyword, and each one has to justify itself against the "every keyword must mean something" constraint or be reframed as a string-labelled construct. The dual role of `week` (reserved word in `plan week`, time unit in `2.5 kg/week`) is the canonical example — none of the three AI models in E1 modelled it correctly. Deciding how to express §2's "control structure" and "user-defined functions" inside a *declarative* DSL without pulling in imperative ceremony was the real design question: a function that returns nothing makes no sense in a language that only describes plans, so the routine-template / call shape was the right idiom but it took the E1 experiment and a re-read of Sebesta §9.4 to commit to it. Building the §4.3 grammar so that the §2 extensions slot in as new alternatives with disjoint FIRST sets (rather than as edits to existing productions) was non-trivial — `<when-decl>`'s optional `else` lookahead and `<routine-decl>`'s position before `<body-sections>` both required redoing the LL(1) inspection in §4.3.5.

## What I plan to fix in Part 2

§4.4 needs the operational rule for `<progress-stmt>` (week-indexed unfolding of a base value plus a rate, in the Sebesta §3.5 SOS style) and the static dispatch rule for `<when-decl>`. §4.5 will be the full type system: dimensional unit families, within-family coercion (`70000 g ≡ 70 kg`), cross-family rejection, `<rate>` cancellation when divided by a matching time unit, and `<exercise-decl>` formalised as the structured (record) type. §4.7 will broaden the expression layer to include multiplicative `*` and `/` with the precedence/associativity table, parenthesised grouping, and the operand-evaluation-order story; the `IDENT` resolution path will then connect routine parameters to the body's `<quantity>` slots so `use "Base Press"(82 kg, 22 kg)` substitutes properly. §4.8 will write up the design rationale per major decision with named trade-offs. On the implementation side: a tree-walking interpreter, the type checker enforcing §4.5, and a "plan one week" output mode that prints the schedule resolved against per-day macro and weight-progression numbers.
